Seven Days, Seven Visualizations is a series of data experiments created by Vizzuality to celebrate and showcase the world’s protected areas. Experts from all over the world are coming together in Sydney for one week at the World Parks Congress, a once-in-a-decade event that sets the agenda for protecting the world’s most valuable places.

Starting Wednesday 12th November (the opening day of the congress), we will release a different visualisation every day to illustrate the various aspects that make the world’s protected areas so special. We have combined open data from the World Database on Protected Areas, the IUCN Red List and many other sources to create each one. Come back every day at 8:30am AEDT time (timezone) to see the latest visualisation. For more information reach contact@vizzuality.com

About 7 visualization in 7 days

Visualization 1: The Expansion of Protected Areas

This visualization takes data on the status date and location of every protected area in the world from the World Database on Protected Areas. Using Odyssey.js we turned this into an illustration of the expansion in protected areas over time, especially in the last 50 years since the first World Parks Congress in 1962.

Data from: ProtectedPlanet.net

Visualization 2: Who’s watching the World Parks Congress?

There are estimated to be 4,000 people at the World Parks Congress in Sydney, and there are thousands more watching it happen at home over the internet. This visualization takes data from Twitter to show who is talking about the World Parks Congress and its events, and visualises it using CartoDB.

Data from: https://simbiotica.cartodb.com/tables/twitter_worldparkscongress/public/map

Visualization 3: Biodiversity data: not just a hobby

Data on the species occurring in protected areas has been amassed by scientists and citizens alike over the years. Using data for bird observations from GBIF we have created a visualization that compares the contributions of academic researchers and citizen observations to our knowledge of bird distributions.

Data from: http://www.gbif.org/occurrence
